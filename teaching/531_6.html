The challenge

  • In rule-and-constraint based phonology, to describe a language you need…
    • an ordered list of rules
    • a list of URs (the lexicon)
    • a phoneme inventory (Steriade 2007 calls this an “underlying alphabet”)
    • maybe restrictions on combinations of segments in URs (called Morpheme Structure Constraint (MSCs))
    • maybe restrictions on the segments allowed in outputs or derivations
      • called “Wellformedness constraints” or “Surface phonetic constraints”
      • Steriade (2007) talks about this with respect Structure preservation
  • The fact that there are so restrictions above seem redundant has been called the Duplication Problem.
  • In OT, all you have is…
    • a list of URs (the lexicon)
    • a ranked list of constraints
    • The lack of an underlying alphabet – or any constraints on the structure of URs – is a property called Richness of the Base.
  • So how can we account for which sounds do and don’t contrast, without stating the phoneme inventory?
    • Especially given Richness of the Base…
  • Contrast in OT comes from faithfulness constraints, which preserve contrast in a certain environment.

Complementary distribution between sounds (no contrast)

  • Imagine a language where p and b are allophones of a single phoneme. In rule terms, /p/ → [b] / V__V no phoneme /b/ → [p] elsewhere
  • Translate into OT using:
    • *V[-voice]V *[+voice,-son] IDENT(voice) probable real input /ap/ ‘dog’
      a [ap]
      b [ab]
      probable real input /ap+a/ ‘dogs’
      c [apa]
      d [aba]
      hypothetical input /ab/ ‘dog’
      e [ap]
      f [ab]
      hypothetical input /ab+a/ ‘dogs’
      g [apa]
      h [aba]
  • /p/ and /b/ don’t contrast, because the grammar won’t let you have a minimal pair.
  • What is it about the ranking that makes that so?

Full contrast between sounds

  • Imagine a different language, where /p/ and /b/ are separate phonemes, with no voicing alternations.
  • Same constraints
    • *V[-voice]V
    • *[+voice,-son]
    • IDENT(voice)
  • Find the ranking: /ap/ ‘dog’
    a [ap]
    b [ab]
    /ap+a/ ‘dogs’
    c [apa]
    d [aba]
    /ab/ ‘cat’
    e [ap]
    f [ab]
    /ab+a/ ‘cats’
    g [apa]
    h [aba]
  • /p/ and /b/ contrast fully-you can get a minimal pair in any environment.
  • What is it about the ranking that makes that so?

Contextually limited contrast (positional neutralization)

  • Example: coda devoicing in German
    • In onsets, obstruents contrast in voicing
    • That contrast does not occur in coda position
    • Results in both positional neutralization and contextually-limited contrast.
  • Two approaches in OT:
    • Context-specific markedness constraints
      • Cause neutralization in a particular environment
      • Example: *Coda-Voice
    • Contextually-limited faithfulness constraints
      • Preserve contrast in a particular environment
      • Example: Ident(voice)-Onset

Context-specific markedness

  • Imagine language with a voicing contrast (phonemes /p/ and /b/), but also intervocalic voicing. In rule terms: /p/ → [b] / V__V /b/ → [b] everywhere → [p] elsewhere

  • Same constraints

    • *V[-voice]V
    • *[+voice,-son]
    • IDENT(voice) /ap/ ‘dog’
      a [ap]
      b [ab]
      /ap+a/ ‘dogs’
      c [apa]
      d [aba]
      /ab/ ‘cat’
      e [ap]
      f [ab]
      /ab+a/ ‘cats’
      g [apa]
      h [aba]

  • /p/ & /b/ contrast in some environments-but in other environments, you get neutralization

  • What is it about the ranking that makes that so?

Contextually-limited contrast as contextual faithfulness

  • Beckman (1998) takes another approach to contextually limited contrast
    • She proposes positional faithfulness constraints, now standard in OT analyses.
    • Contrast is preserved in a context X because a faithfulness targets context
    • Common contexts where contrast is preserved: stressed syllables and initial syllables.
  • Preservation in initial syllables is captured by the constraint below (where F is replaced by some feature): (adapted from Beckman, emphasis added)

IDENT-SYLL1(F) Let Y be an output segment in the root-initial syllable, and X its input correspondent. IF Y is [αF] then X must be [αF].

  • If IDENT-SYLL1(F) >> MARKEDNESS >> IDENT(F), we get contextually limited contrast

Lexicon optimization

  • Does Richness of the Base mean anything can be in underlying representations?
    • In theory, yes – there are no hard limits on URs.
    • For an actual language, no – learners won’t posit “weird” or “crazy” URs without evidence.
  • Prince & Smolensky (1993/2004) suggest a learning proceddure called lexicon optimization.
    • Basically, the learner only posits URs that deviate from SRs when there is sufficient motivation (such as an alternation).
    • In the absence of such evidence, the UR should match the SR.
    • This runs contrary to the idea that we should minimize the number of underlying sounds in a language.
  • Steriade (2007): “The idea is that non-alternating phonological properties - say the aspiration of initial [kʰ] in [kʰæt] - are always present in the lexical entry, for the following reason: the right grammar will guarantee the surface occurrence of [kʰ] in [kʰæt] no matter whether [kʰ] or [k] is present underlyingly, but the advantage of the identity mapping /kʰæt/ ➞ [kʰæt] over /kæt/ ➞ [kæt] is that the former avoids a faithfulness violation (Ident [aspiration]). Lexicon Optimization yields (approximately) opposite results on the issue of underlying specification compared to lexical minimality: non-alternating redundant information will always end up lexically listed.”
